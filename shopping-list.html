Build Instructions

1
Tank Structure — Day 1
Plan your layout

Divide the tank roughly 60% land / 40% water. Water section on one side, not the center — this gives you a natural shoreline and more usable land area.

Install the divider

Cut plexiglass to fit tank width, about 5–6" tall. Silicone it in place at the 40% mark. Let cure 24 hours. Doesn't need to be perfectly watertight — slight seepage is fine.

Build the water section

Add 1–1.5" of aquarium gravel/sand on the water side. Place rocks for visual interest and shrimp hiding spots. Create a gradual slope toward the divider.

Build the land drainage layers

Stack in order on the land side:

  1. LECA — 1.5" deep across entire land section
  2. Mesh/landscape fabric — cut to fit, laid flat
  3. Activated charcoal — thin layer (~0.5")
  4. ABG soil mix — 2–3" deep, higher at back, sloping toward water
Add hardscape

Press driftwood and cork bark into the soil. Create caves/overhangs with cork bark for isopod and spider hides. Place rocks along the shoreline. Make sure driftwood has a prominent perch — the spider will use it as a hunting lookout.

2
Planting — Day 2
Plant the land section (back to front)

Back: Marcgravia against the back glass (it'll shingle up over time), peacock begonia for height.

Middle: Jewel orchid as centerpiece, fittonia around it for pops of pink/white, ruby red selaginella for contrast.

Front/shoreline: Baby tears as ground carpet, mini bromeliad near water's edge.

Ground cover: Press mood moss and fern moss into all bare soil — mix both for texture.

Forest floor: Scatter dried leaf litter around cork bark and between plants.

Plant the water section

Tie java moss to rocks. Attach bucephalandra to rocks (don't bury rhizome). Plant rotala in gravel at the back. Drop marimo balls onto gravel. Add a tiny pinch of duckweed to the surface.

Add water

Mix dechlorinated water with marine aquarium salt (~1 tbsp per gallon). Slowly fill the water section to 1" below divider top. Mist the entire land section until soil is damp but not waterlogged.

Wait

Let the fully planted tank sit for 48 hours before adding any animals. This lets water chemistry stabilize and plants settle in.

3
Animal Introduction — Days 4–21

Order matters — prey populations must be booming before predators go in.

Day 4
Microfauna: Spread springtails across land (focus on leaf litter/moss). Release Dairy Cow isopods under cork bark. Add ostracod + daphnia cultures to water.
Day 7
Snails & scuds: Add ramshorn + bladder snails to water. Add land snails to land section. Release scuds into water.
Day 10
Shrimp & assassins: Float opae ula in shipping water for 30 min to acclimate, then release. Add assassin snails to water.
Day 14
Fruit flies: Introduce flightless fruit fly culture to land. Place a small piece of overripe banana under a leaf to kickstart breeding.
Day 21
Apex predators: Scatter pseudoscorpions across land near leaf litter. Gently place the jumping spider on driftwood or cork bark.
4
Seal & Monitor — Day 21+
Seal the tank

Once all animals are active, seal the glass lid with aquarium-safe silicone. Fully airtight. Plants produce O₂, animals produce CO₂ — the cycle sustains itself.

Position and light

Place in indirect natural light — never direct sunlight (overheats and kills everything). Set LED grow light on timer: 10–12 hours/day. This is the only ongoing input.

Monitor for 2–4 weeks

After 4–6 weeks of stable conditions (steady condensation, clear water, active animals, growing plants) — walk away and enjoy forever.

5
How the Ecosystem Works
▶Plants photosynthesize — produce O₂, absorb CO₂
▶Water evaporates — condenses on glass — drips back down (rain cycle)
▶Springtails & isopods eat dead matter — produce nutrients for plants
▶Aquatic plants oxygenate water — absorb ammonia and waste
▶Opae ula shrimp graze algae — keep water crystal clear
▶Ramshorn & bladder snails breed + eat algae — hunted by assassin snails
▶Assassin snails regulate snail populations — prevent overpopulation
▶Daphnia & scuds eat microorganisms — maintain water clarity
▶Fruit flies breed on decaying matter — hunted by the spider
▶Jumping spider hunts flies, springtails, isopods — land apex predator
▶Pseudoscorpions hunt springtails/mites — permanent backup apex
▶Everything that dies gets consumed — nutrients cycle back to plants

Important Notes

Spider lifespan: Bold jumping spiders live ~8–14 months. When it dies, springtails and isopods consume the body. Pseudoscorpions seamlessly continue as apex predator. You can unseal and add a new spider if you want — but the ecosystem keeps running without one.

Opae ula water: These need slightly brackish (salty) water. The 1 tbsp marine salt per gallon is critical — add during setup before any animals go in. Other aquatic animals tolerate light brackish.

Water volume: Aim for 4–5 gallons of actual water. More water = more stable chemistry.

Temperature: Room temp (65–80°F / 18–27°C). Avoid heating vents, AC, and direct-sun windows.

Fruit fly colony: Naturally dropping/dying leaves sustain them long-term. Initial banana piece just kickstarts the colony.

Grow light tip: Jewel orchid and peacock begonia look best under moderate indirect light — position the grow light slightly off to the side for the best iridescence effect.

Where to buy: Search "bioactive terrarium starter kit" for springtail + isopod bundles, "opae ula shrimp" from dedicated shrimp breeders, "Phidippus audax" from jumping spider breeders, and assassin snails at aquarium stores.
