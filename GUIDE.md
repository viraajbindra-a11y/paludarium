## BUILD INSTRUCTIONS

### Phase 1: Tank Structure (Day 1)

**Step 1 — Plan your layout.**
Divide the tank roughly 60% land / 40% water. Put the water section on one side, not the center — this gives you a natural-looking shoreline and more usable land area for plants and the spider.

**Step 2 — Install the divider.**
Cut the plexiglass to fit your tank width. It should be about 5–6 inches tall. Silicone it in place about 40% from one end. Let the silicone cure for a full 24 hours. The divider doesn't need to be perfectly watertight — slight seepage into the land drainage layer is fine and actually helps keep soil moisture balanced.

**Step 3 — Build the water section.**
On the water side, add 1–1.5 inches of fine aquarium gravel or sand. Place a few small rocks for visual interest and shrimp hiding spots. Create a gradual slope up toward the divider to make the shoreline look natural.

**Step 4 — Build the land drainage layers.**
On the land side, stack these layers in order:

1. **LECA** — 1.5 inches deep across the entire land section
2. **Mesh/landscape fabric** — cut to fit, laid flat on top of LECA
3. **Activated charcoal** — thin layer (~0.5 inch) on top of mesh
4. **ABG soil mix** — 2–3 inches deep

Shape the soil higher at the back, sloping down toward the water. This looks more natural and helps drainage.

**Step 5 — Add hardscape.**
Press driftwood and cork bark into the land soil. Create small caves and overhangs with the cork bark — these become isopod hideouts and spider shelters. Place rocks along the shoreline for a natural land-to-water transition. Make sure the driftwood has a prominent perch spot — your spider will use it as a hunting lookout.

### Phase 2: Planting (Day 2)

**Step 6 — Plant the land section.**
Work from back to front:

- **Back:** Marcgravia against the back glass (it will shingle up the wall over time), peacock begonia for height
- **Middle:** Jewel orchid as a centerpiece, fittonia around it for pops of pink/white, ruby red selaginella for contrast
- **Front/shoreline:** Baby tears as ground carpet, miniature bromeliad near the water's edge
- **Ground cover:** Press mood moss and fern moss into all bare soil. Mix the two types for texture contrast
- **Forest floor:** Scatter dried leaf litter around the cork bark and between plants — this is food for the cleanup crew and hunting ground for the spider

**Step 7 — Plant the water section.**

- Tie java moss to rocks or small driftwood pieces
- Attach bucephalandra to rocks (don't bury the rhizome)
- Plant rotala in the gravel at the back of the water section
- Drop marimo moss balls onto the gravel
- Add a very small pinch of duckweed to the surface (it spreads fast — less is more)

**Step 8 — Add water.**
Mix dechlorinated water with marine aquarium salt — about 1 tablespoon per gallon (this is for the opae ula shrimp). Slowly fill the water section to about 1 inch below the divider top. Then mist the entire land section thoroughly with the spray bottle until the soil is damp but not waterlogged.

**Let the fully planted tank sit for 48 hours before adding any animals.** This lets the water chemistry stabilize and the plants settle in.

### Phase 3: Animal Introduction (Days 4–21)

Order matters — prey populations must be booming before predators go in.

**Day 4 — Microfauna (the foundation)**

- Spread the **springtail culture** across the entire land section — focus on leaf litter, moss, and around cork bark
- Release **Dairy Cow isopods** under cork bark and near leaf litter
- Pour **ostracod culture** into the water section
- Add **daphnia culture** to the water

**Day 7 — Snails and scuds**

- Add **ramshorn snails** (8–10) and **bladder snails** (8–10) to the water — these are both algae cleaners AND prey for the assassin snails
- Add **small land snails** (5–8) to the land section
- Release **scuds** into the water

**Day 10 — Shrimp and assassin snails**

- **Opae ula shrimp:** Float them in their shipping water in a small container on the tank water surface for 30 minutes (temperature acclimation), then gently release
- Add **assassin snails** (3–5) to the water section — they'll begin hunting ramshorns and bladders, keeping those populations in check

**Day 14 — Fruit flies**

- Introduce the **flightless fruit fly culture** onto the land section
- Place a small piece of overripe banana under a leaf to kickstart a breeding colony
- The banana will decompose naturally — springtails will clean up the remains while fruit flies breed

**Day 21 — The apex predators**

- Release **pseudoscorpions** (5–10) across the land section, scattering them near leaf litter and cork bark. They'll immediately start hunting springtails and settling in.
- Gently place your **bold jumping spider** on the driftwood or cork bark. It will explore and find its preferred perch within hours.

### Phase 4: Seal and Monitor (Day 21+)

**Step 9 — Seal the tank.**
Once all animals are introduced and you can see activity (spider hunting, shrimp grazing, springtails swarming leaf litter), seal the glass lid with aquarium-safe silicone. You want it fully airtight. The plants produce oxygen, the animals produce CO2 — the cycle sustains itself.

**Step 10 — Position and light.**
Place the tank somewhere with **indirect natural light** — never direct sunlight, which will overheat the tank and kill everything. Set the LED grow light on a timer for **10–12 hours per day**. This is the only ongoing input the system needs.

**Step 11 — Monitor for 2–4 weeks.**

| What You See | What It Means | What to Do |
|-------------|---------------|------------|
| Light condensation on glass in the morning | Water cycle is working perfectly | Nothing — this is ideal |
| Heavy fog / can't see through the glass | Too much moisture | Briefly unseal, let some moisture escape, reseal |
| No condensation at all | Too dry | Briefly unseal, mist lightly, reseal |
| White mold on soil or wood | Normal for new terrariums — springtails eat it | Wait it out. They'll handle it within a week or two |
| Plants yellowing | Light is too strong or too weak | Adjust grow light distance or timer hours |
| Spider building a web sac in a corner | It's settled in and comfortable | Good sign — it has a home base |

**After 4–6 weeks of stable conditions, you're done. Walk away and enjoy.**

### Phase 5: The Ecosystem at Work

Here's what's happening inside your sealed world:

- **Plants** photosynthesize → produce oxygen, absorb CO2
- **Water** evaporates → condenses on glass → drips back down (rain cycle)
- **Springtails & isopods** eat dead plant matter and waste → produce nutrients for plants
- **Aquatic plants** oxygenate water → absorb ammonia and waste
- **Opae ula shrimp** graze algae → keep water clear
- **Ramshorn & bladder snails** breed → eat algae → get hunted by assassin snails
- **Assassin snails** regulate snail populations → prevent overpopulation
- **Daphnia & scuds** eat microorganisms → maintain water clarity
- **Fruit flies** breed on decaying matter → get hunted by the spider
- **Jumping spider** hunts fruit flies, springtails, small isopods → apex land predator
- **Pseudoscorpions** hunt springtails and mites → long-term backup apex
- **Everything that dies** gets consumed by springtails and isopods → nutrients cycle back to plants

## IMPORTANT NOTES

**Spider lifespan:** Bold jumping spiders live roughly 8–14 months. When yours dies, its body will be consumed by springtails and isopods — nothing goes to waste, and it feeds the cycle. The pseudoscorpions will seamlessly continue as the apex predator. If you ever want another spider, you can unseal, add one, and reseal — but you don't *have* to. The ecosystem will keep running without it.

**Opae ula water:** These shrimp need slightly brackish (salty) water. The 1 tablespoon of marine salt per gallon is critical — add it during setup, before any animals go in. The other aquatic animals (scuds, daphnia, snails) all tolerate light brackish conditions.

**Water volume:** Aim for 4–5 gallons of actual water in the aquatic section. More water = more stable chemistry.

**Temperature:** Keep at normal room temperature (65–80°F / 18–27°C). Avoid heating vents, AC units, and direct-sun windows.

**Fruit fly colony:** The flightless flies breed in decaying organic matter. Naturally dropping/dying leaves in the terrarium sustain them long-term. The initial banana piece is just to kickstart the colony.

**Where to buy:** Every item in the shopping list above has a direct buy link. Key sources: [Amazon](https://amazon.com) for hardware, substrates, and common species; [Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=terrarium+supplies) for rare plants and invertebrates; [Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=bucephalandra+aquarium) for aquatic plants; [Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=terrarium+plants) for terrarium plants, isopods, and springtails; [Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=reptile+terrarium+supplies) for spiders and pseudoscorpions; [Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=aquatic+arts+live) for snails and aquatic animals.

**Grow light note:** The jewel orchid and peacock begonia look their best under moderate indirect light — too much direct light washes out the iridescence. Position the grow light slightly off to the side rather than directly overhead for the best visual effect.
